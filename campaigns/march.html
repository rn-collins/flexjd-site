Why this campaign

The Women's History Month 2027 theme — 'Financial Literacy, Liberation, and Leadership: Women in Business' — is tailor-made for a law school. Financial liberation and the 'history of women's economic rights and landmark legislation' are the thread connecting suffrage to the Equal Pay Act to MEPA to the MA Pay Transparency Law. And this year, that theme lands against a legal backdrop defined by: the MA Pay Transparency Law taking effect (October 29, 2025), the PWFA upheld by the 5th Circuit, the gender pay gap widening for the second consecutive year, and the Title IX 2024 rule vacated. The campaign has both celebratory and urgent content to offer.

The campaign's NUSL anchor is the Judith Olans Brown Forum for Women in the Law — 19th Annual, May 14, 2027. The 2026–27 Practitioner-in-Residence is Celina Stewart, CEO of the League of Women Voters. These are usable, immediate assets.

The legal framework

KEY WOMEN'S RIGHTS LEGAL MILESTONES
1848 — Seneca Falls Declaration
The 'Declaration of Sentiments' modeled on the Declaration of Independence; began the 72-year campaign for women's suffrage.
1869 — Arabella Mansfield admitted to the bar
First woman admitted to the bar in the United States (Iowa). Made possible by a state statute — not by any right recognized at common law.
1879 — Belva Lockwood
First woman to argue before the U.S. Supreme Court.
1920 — 19th Amendment
Women's right to vote — after 72 years of organizing from Seneca Falls.
1931 — Sadie T.M. Alexander
First Black woman to earn a PhD in economics in the US and the first Black woman to graduate from Penn Law and be admitted to the Pennsylvania bar. Her work sits at the intersection of race, gender, and economic rights, connecting directly to WHM's economic-justice themes.
1963 — Equal Pay Act
Prohibits wage differentials based on sex for substantially equal work performed under similar conditions. Exceptions historically limited its reach.
1964 — Title VII (Civil Rights Act)
Prohibits employment discrimination based on sex (among other protected characteristics). Interpreted by SCOTUS in Bostock (2020) to cover sexual orientation and gender identity.
1971 — Reed v. Reed
First SCOTUS application of Equal Protection to sex discrimination: Idaho statute preferring male estate administrators over female was struck. Justice Burger majority; Ruth Bader Ginsburg briefed the case for the ACLU Women's Rights Project (her first SCOTUS brief).
1972 — Title IX
Prohibits sex discrimination in educational programs receiving federal funding. See Section 5 for current Title IX status.
1973 — Frontiero v. Richardson
8-1: federal law requiring male military members to prove their spouses' financial dependence (while female members got automatic benefits for husbands) was unconstitutional. Brennan plurality applied strict scrutiny to sex; Powell plurality declined. The case RBG argued for the ACLU that came closest to strict scrutiny before Craig v. Boren (1976) established intermediate scrutiny.
1978 — Pregnancy Discrimination Act
Added pregnancy to Title VII's prohibitions. Led directly to the 2022 PWFA.
1981 — Sandra Day O'Connor
First woman on the U.S. Supreme Court (d. December 1, 2023). Her legacy: the swing vote on abortion (Planned Parenthood v. Casey, 1992), affirmative action (Grutter, 2003), and detainee rights.
1994 — VAWA
Violence Against Women Act; reauthorized 2022.
1996 — United States v. Virginia (VMI case)
7-1, Ginsburg: Virginia Military Institute's male-only admissions policy violated Equal Protection. Required states to have an 'exceedingly persuasive justification' for sex classifications.
2009 — Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act
First bill signed by President Obama. Resets the 180-day filing clock for pay discrimination claims with each new discriminatory paycheck — overruling SCOTUS's Ledbetter v. Goodyear (2007) decision.
2022 — Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson confirmed
First Black woman on the U.S. Supreme Court. Harvard AB 1992, Harvard Law 1996. Former Public Defender.
2022 — Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization
6-3, Alito: Overruled Roe v. Wade (1973) and Planned Parenthood v. Casey (1992). Returned abortion regulation to the states.
2022 — PWFA signed (eff. June 18, 2024)
Pregnant Workers Fairness Act — see Section 5.

CRITICAL LEGAL DEVELOPMENTS
Equal Rights Amendment — status
⚠️ STILL UNPUBLISHED. On December 17, 2024, Archivist Colleen Shogan and Deputy Archivist William Bosanko stated the ERA 'cannot be certified as part of the Constitution due to established legal, judicial, and procedural decisions,' citing 2020 and 2022 OLC opinions that the ratification deadline is valid. President Biden declared it 'the law of the land' on January 17, 2025 — no legal force. Ongoing litigation: Equal Means Equal v. Trump (D. Mass., Judge William G. Young; arguments March 24, 2026). Prof. Davis (NUSL): Biden's statement 'does move the ball … but it doesn't end the controversy.' Status current as of publication; confirm before relying on it for time-sensitive advocacy.
Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA) — UPHELD 2025
EEOC final rule effective June 18, 2024 (employers with 15+ employees; reasonable accommodation for known limitations from pregnancy, childbirth, or related conditions absent undue hardship). / ★ 5th Circuit upheld the PWFA's constitutionality on August 15, 2025 (Texas v. Bondi) — reversed the district court and rejected the Quorum Clause challenge. / ⚠️ CAVEAT: A Louisiana federal judge in May 2025 vacated the EEOC rule portions requiring accommodation for elective abortions. The Trump EEOC has signaled intent to rescind Biden-era regulations. Net: PWFA itself is in full effect nationwide; abortion-accommodation provisions are vacated. Confirm current EEOC guidance before relying on this for time-sensitive advocacy.
Title IX — 2020 rules reinstated
The 2024 Title IX Final Rule (which expressly covered dating violence, gender identity, and sex stereotypes) was vacated nationwide on January 9, 2025 (Tennessee v. Cardona, E.D. Ky., Judge Reeves — found the rule exceeded the Department's authority and violated the First Amendment). The 2020 Title IX regulations are now restored nationwide. The 2024 rule had expanded 'sex-based harassment' to include sexual orientation, gender identity, sex stereotypes, and pregnancy. The current standard: 'severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive'; formal complaint process; deliberate-indifference liability. Title IX Coordinator at NUSL: Mark Jannoni (m.jannoni@northeastern.edu; Richards Hall 125; 617-373-3543).
Dobbs aftermath — national map
Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization (2022, 6-3, Alito) overruled Roe and Casey. The Guttmacher Institute's state-policy tracker is the authoritative, frequently updated source — cite its CURRENT figure at campaign launch rather than a fixed number (the map changes as court decisions come in). FDA v. Alliance for Hippocratic Medicine (2024): SCOTUS preserved mifepristone access on standing grounds.

Massachusetts reproductive protections
ROE Act (2020): codified abortion rights, allowed later abortions for fatal fetal diagnoses, removed barriers for 16–17-year-olds. Shield Law (St. 2022 c. 127): limited MA cooperation with out-of-state investigations. Shield Act 2.0 (St. 2025 c. 16, signed Aug. 7, 2025): strongest reproductive-care shield law in America — strengthens provider data privacy, formally establishes emergency abortion access, and bars MA cooperation with out-of-state or federal investigations into protected care. Abortion is legal in MA generally to 24 weeks.
MA Pay Transparency Law (Chapter 141/2024)
Employers with 25+ MA employees: must disclose salary ranges in job postings and on request (eff. Oct. 29, 2025). Employers with 100+: must file annual EEO wage-data reports (first due Feb. 1, 2025). AG-only enforcement; NO private right of action. A major addition for 2027 campaign content — ties directly to the WHM 2027 'Financial Liberation' theme.
MA Equal Pay Act (MEPA, G.L. c. 149 §105A)
Effective July 1, 2018. 'Comparable work' standard (broader than federal EPA's 'equal work'). 2x liquidated damages plus attorneys' fees. Good-faith self-evaluation affirmative defense. MA women earn ~83¢/$1 vs. men; Black women ~58¢/$1; Latinas ~50¢/$1.
CURRENT DATA ON WOMEN IN THE LEGAL PROFESSION
The gender pay gap widened for the SECOND CONSECUTIVE YEAR in 2024 — women working full-time were paid 81¢/$1 (down from 83¢ in 2023 and 84¢ in 2022). The first time since this data became available in 1960 that the gap widened two consecutive years.
National Women's Law Center analysis of US Census Bureau data, 2024. nwlc.org.
Intersectional wage gap (relative to white non-Hispanic men): white non-Hispanic women 73¢; Black women 63¢; Latinas 54¢; Native American women 53¢.
National Partnership for Women & Families, 2024 wage data. nationalpartnership.org.
NWLC estimates the pay gap costs women collectively almost $1.9 trillion per year.
National Women's Law Center. nwlc.org.
Women are 41% of all US lawyers (up from 36% in 2014); 56.2% of law students; but only 28% of law firm partners; 12% of managing partners.
ABA Profile of the Legal Profession 2024 (6th annual, released November 2024). americanbar.org.
