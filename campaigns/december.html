Why this campaign

NUSL is ranked #2 nationally for public interest law (preLaw 2025) and has received an A+ rating for human rights. But the deeper story is what NUSL faculty and programs have actually built: a university-confirmed institution with five active Centers of Excellence doing documented human-rights work, from investigating racial homicides in the Jim Crow South to representing asylum-seekers detained at the border, to advising states on the right to water. Human Rights Day is the one campaign where NUSL's own institutional record is the most compelling content available.

KEY STATISTICS — HUMAN RIGHTS IN THE US
US incarceration rate: 541 per 100,000 (5th highest globally)
Bureau of Justice Statistics (2022 data via World Prison Brief, September 3, 2025). The Prison Policy Initiative estimates nearly 2 million people incarcerated and roughly $445 billion annual system cost. Every US state incarcerates women at a higher per-capita rate than most independent nations; the US holds approximately one-quarter of the world's incarcerated women.

HUMAN RIGHTS DAY — CONFIRMED DATES AND THEME PLANNING
Date
December 10 — always. All of December is Human Rights Month.
Human Rights Day vs. World Day of Human Rights
These are the SAME observance — December 10. South Africa has a separate national Human Rights Day on March 21 (anniversary of the 1960 Sharpeville massacre). No other distinction.
2025 theme (confirmed)

OHCHR already has a dedicated '2026: Marking the 60th anniversary of the ICCPR and ICESCR' page. This is the safe planning hook.

UNIVERSAL DECLARATION OF HUMAN RIGHTS — KEY FACTS FOR CAMPAIGN CONTENT
Adoption
December 10, 1948, Palais de Chaillot, Paris. UNGA Resolution 217 A (III). / Human Rights Day formally established by UNGA Resolution 423 (V), 1950; first observed December 10, 1950.
Vote
48 in favor | 0 against | 8 abstentions: Byelorussia, Czechoslovakia, Poland, Saudi Arabia, Ukraine, the Union of South Africa, the USSR, and Yugoslavia. / Honduras and Yemen were absent. No country voted against. / Context: South Africa abstained to protect apartheid; Saudi Arabia objected to equal marriage and religious-freedom provisions; the Soviet bloc wanted stronger economic-rights and anti-fascism language.
Key drafters
Eleanor Roosevelt (US) — chaired the 18-member UN Commission on Human Rights drafting committee. / René Cassin (France) — prepared the first formal draft; compared the Declaration to a Greek temple (universality resting on dignity and equality as its pillars). / Charles Malik (Lebanon) — rapporteur. / P.C. Chang (China) — vice-chair; championed pluralism and non-Western philosophical traditions. / John Humphrey (Canada, UN Secretariat) — drafted the initial blueprint from his survey of existing human-rights instruments. / Hansa Mehta (India) — secured gender-inclusive language ('all human beings' rather than 'all men'). / Hernán Santa Cruz (Chile) — pushed successfully for economic and social rights alongside civil and political rights. / Drafting process: 1947–1948.
Structure of the 30 Articles
Articles 1–2: Foundational (dignity, freedom, equality, non-discrimination — 'born free and equal in dignity and rights') / Articles 3–21: Civil and political rights (life, liberty, fair trial, privacy, expression, assembly, political participation, nationality) / Articles 22–27: Economic, social, and cultural rights (work, fair wages, rest, standard of living, education, social security, health, cultural life) / Articles 28–30: Framework (right to a social and international order in which rights can be realized; duties to the community; no right may be used to destroy other rights)
The International Bill of Human Rights
The UDHR + the ICCPR (1966) + the ICESCR (1966) together form the 'International Bill of Human Rights.' The two Covenants entered into force in 1976, making the aspirational language of the UDHR legally binding for ratifying states.
US ratification status — key teaching point for law students
RATIFIED: ICCPR (1992, with 5 reservations, 5 understandings, 4 declarations, AND a critical declaration that Arts. 1–27 are 'not self-executing' — meaning no private right of action in US courts); Genocide Convention (1988); CERD (1994); Convention Against Torture (1994); ICCPR had 175 parties as of December 2025. / SIGNED BUT NOT RATIFIED: ICESCR (signed 1977, never ratified — the US is not legally bound); CEDAW (signed 1980, never ratified — the US is one of only a handful of countries not to have ratified it); CRPD (signed 2009, not ratified). / NOT RATIFIED, ONLY SIGNED (shameful distinction): The US is the ONLY UN member state that has NOT ratified the Convention on the Rights of the Child. Somalia ratified in 2015. The US remains alone. / Why this matters: the non-self-executing declaration on the ICCPR means that even where the US has ratified a treaty, domestic courts generally cannot enforce it unless Congress passes implementing legislation.
Universal Periodic Review (UPR)
A peer-review mechanism of the UN Human Rights Council (established 2006, GA Res. 60/251). Every UN member state is reviewed against the Charter, UDHR, ratified treaties, and IHL roughly every 4.5 years. The 4th cycle began November 2022. See Section 5 for the unprecedented 2025 US boycott.
US WITHDRAWAL FROM INTERNATIONAL HUMAN-RIGHTS MECHANISMS
Executive Order 14199 (Feb. 4, 2025) — HRC withdrawal
Withdrew the US from and ended US funding to the UN Human Rights Council and UNRWA. / ⚠️ PRECISE FRAMING: The US's elected 2022–2024 HRC term expired December 31, 2024, so the US already held only observer status when EO 14199 was signed. Accurate framing: the Biden administration held a full elected member seat (2022–2024); that term expired; EO 14199 then formally ended US observer participation and terminated all HRC funding. Do NOT say 'the US withdrew from the HRC' as if it held a current seat — that is technically imprecise and easily rebutted.
EO 14203 (Feb. 2025) — ICC sanctions
Imposed OFAC sanctions on International Criminal Court personnel. On February 13, 2025, OFAC designated ICC Prosecutor Karim Khan. UN human-rights experts called the order 'an attack on global rule of law' that 'strikes at the very heart of the international criminal justice system.'
July 9, 2025 — UN Special Rapporteur sanctioned
Secretary of State Marco Rubio sanctioned UN Special Rapporteur Francesca Albanese. UN High Commissioner for Human Rights Volker Türk (statement July 10, 2025) urged 'the prompt reversal of U.S. sanctions against a Special Rapporteur,' warning that 'attacks and threats against Special Procedures mandate holders … must stop.' Source: ohchr.org/en/press-releases/2025/07/comment-un-high-commissioner-human-rights-volker-turk-us-sanctions-against
November 7, 2025 — US boycotts its own UPR
The US was scheduled for its 4th-cycle Universal Periodic Review on November 7, 2025. In August 2025, the administration announced it would not participate and did not submit a national report. This is the FIRST TIME in UPR history (the mechanism has existed since 2006) that a UN member state has declined to participate in its own review. The Human Rights Council adopted a decision urging the US to resume cooperation and rescheduled the review for 2026. More than 150 organizations had submitted stakeholder reports. / Why this matters for law students: the UPR is a peer-accountability mechanism that applies equally to all states regardless of wealth or power. The US boycott creates a gap in the accountability chain that US lawyers — including human-rights advocates — must understand.
Human Rights Watch World Report 2026 (released Feb. 4, 2026)
Executive Director Philippe Bolopion: 'The global human rights system is in peril. Under relentless pressure from US President Donald Trump, and persistently undermined by China and Russia, the rules-based international order is being crushed.' Report documents: 72% of the world's population now living under autocracy (back to 1985 levels by some metrics); 32 deaths in ICE custody in 2025 (+4 by mid-January 2026); termination of nearly all US foreign aid; dismantling of USAID. hrw.org/world-report/2026
WHY US LAWYERS SHOULD ENGAGE WITH INTERNATIONAL HUMAN RIGHTS LAW
What is human rights law vs. civil rights law?
Civil rights law typically refers to constitutional and statutory protections against discrimination in specific contexts (employment, housing, public accommodations) under US federal and state law. Human rights law refers to the body of international norms — treaties, customary international law, and UDHR principles — that apply to every person by virtue of their humanity, without requiring state ratification. The US civil rights framework is substantially narrower: it does not recognize economic rights (right to food, housing, healthcare) as legal entitlements, and it has limited mechanisms for enforcing rights extraterritorially. Human rights law provides a parallel and often more expansive framework.
What use are unratified treaties in US practice?
Even where the US has not ratified a treaty (e.g., ICESCR, CEDAW, CRC), these instruments are relevant in: (1) statutory interpretation — courts may look to international norms when statutes are ambiguous; (2) litigation before international bodies — US compliance with ILO conventions, IACHR, etc.; (3) advocacy before Congress and state legislatures; (4) comparative law arguments in appellate litigation (Atkins v. Virginia, Roper v. Simmons, Lawrence v. Texas all cited international consensus); (5) shadow report processes before UN treaty bodies, which produce public accountability even without domestic legal effect.
What does the US's non-self-executing ICCPR declaration mean?
The US ratified the ICCPR in 1992 but simultaneously declared it 'not self-executing,' meaning no US court can enforce ICCPR obligations directly in domestic litigation unless Congress passes implementing legislation. As a matter of treaty law, the US is still internationally bound — it can be reported to the Human Rights Committee and reviewed at the UPR. But the declaration renders the treaty largely non-justiciable in US courts. This is a foundational concept for any student interested in international human-rights practice.
The UPR boycott as a case study in rule-of-law accountability
The UPR is designed so that no state — including the most powerful — can escape periodic accountability. When the US declined to participate in November 2025, the Human Rights Council continued the review anyway, using stakeholder reports submitted by civil society organizations. This case study illustrates both the limits and the resilience of international accountability mechanisms — highly relevant to a public-interest law school audience.
