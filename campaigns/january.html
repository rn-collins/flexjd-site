Why this campaign

Human trafficking is one of the most under-reported and under-prosecuted crimes in the United States. Future lawyers encounter it in immigration, family law, criminal defense, civil litigation, labor and employment law, housing law, and public interest practice. Understanding the TVPA, T visas, U visas, § 1595 civil remedies, Safe Harbor laws, mandatory reporter obligations, and trauma-informed lawyering is not optional — it is foundational for any attorney serving vulnerable populations.

Northeastern has skin in this game. Prof. Amy Farrell is one of the foremost human trafficking researchers in the nation. The Immigrant Justice Clinic represents trafficking survivors on T-visa applications. The Violence and Justice Research Lab and the Center on Crime, Race, and Justice house multiple active NIJ-funded studies. And the Boston legal-services ecosystem — MLMC, GBLS, the MA AG's Human Trafficking Division, the CAT Task Force, SEEN — is among the most developed in the country.

The legal framework

TVPA LINEAGE AND KEY PROVISIONS
TVPA — Trafficking Victims Protection Act (2000) and reauthorizations
Original: Victims of Trafficking and Violence Protection Act of 2000, P.L. 106-386 (22 U.S.C. 7101 et seq.). / Three-pronged framework: PREVENTION, PROTECTION, PROSECUTION. / Reauthorizations: 2003 (added civil remedy); 2005; 2008 (William Wilberforce TVPRA — added beneficiary liability); 2013 (attached to VAWA reauthorization); 2018 (Frederick Douglass Trafficking Victims Prevention and Protection Reauthorization Act); Most recent: Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act of 2022, enacted January 2023 as Pub. L. 117-348.
Core TVPA definition of 'severe forms of trafficking'
(A) SEX TRAFFICKING: the recruitment, harboring, transportation, provision, obtaining, patronizing, or soliciting of a person for the purpose of a commercial sex act — induced by force, fraud, or coercion, OR when the victim is under 18 (no force/fraud/coercion required for minors). / (B) LABOR TRAFFICKING: the recruitment, harboring, transportation, provision, or obtaining of a person for labor or services through force, fraud, or coercion for the purpose of subjection to involuntary servitude, peonage, debt bondage, or slavery. / Critical note: physical transportation is NOT required — trafficking can occur in one location.
18 U.S.C. § 1595 — Civil Remedy (KNOW THIS)
Victims may bring a civil action against: (1) the perpetrator AND (2) any person who 'knowingly benefits, or attempts or conspires to benefit, financially or by receiving anything of value from participation in a venture which that person knew or should have known has engaged in an act in violation of this chapter' — BENEFICIARY LIABILITY. / Damages: compensatory damages + reasonable attorneys' fees. / Statute of limitations: 10 years (amended 2015). / Beneficiary liability added by the 2008 William Wilberforce reauthorization — now the foundation of hotel-liability litigation. / 929 federal civil cases filed under § 1595 from Dec. 19, 2003–Dec. 31, 2023; $900M+ in civil damages and public settlements (Human Trafficking Legal Center, 2023 report).
Palermo Protocol (2000)
UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons, Especially Women and Children — supplements the UN Convention Against Transnational Organized Crime. The source of the force/fraud/coercion definition later adopted in the TVPA. Over 175 state parties. Requires criminalization, victim protection, and international cooperation.
ILO 2014 Protocol to Forced Labour Convention No. 29 (1930)
Modernizes the foundational 1930 ILO Convention. Requires member states to adopt prevention measures, protect victims (including from prosecution for trafficking-related offenses), and ensure access to appropriate and effective remedies and compensation. Ratified by approximately 60 states.
T VISA vs. U VISA — SIDE-BY-SIDE FOR LAW STUDENTS
Who qualifies
T: Victims of 'severe forms of trafficking' as defined by TVPA. | U: Victims of certain qualifying crimes (including trafficking, sexual assault, domestic violence, stalking, and others) who have suffered substantial physical or mental abuse and have been, are being, or are likely to be helpful in investigation/prosecution.
Annual cap
T: 5,000 principal T-1 visas (8 CFR 214.210); family derivatives uncapped. | U: 10,000 principal U-1 visas; derivatives uncapped.
Law enforcement certification
T: NOT required (strongly encouraged as primary evidence; LEA declaration is the preferred but not the only evidence). | U: REQUIRED — Form I-918 Supplement B signed by a law enforcement official. This is the #1 barrier to U visas in practice.
Current application volume and wait
T: FY2024: 15,332 applications received (801% increase since FY2021); 78.2% approval rate; cap reached each year; BFD process provides relief while waiting. | U: FY2024: 41,556 petitions received; 10,000 approved (cap hit); backlog over 249,000; estimated wait 15+ years for final approval.

Bona Fide Determination (BFD) — T visa
DHS Final Rule (89 FR 34864, eff. Aug. 28, 2024): USCIS issues BFD if application appears to be filed in good faith → automatic deferred action + Employment Authorization Document (EAD, category (c)(40)) + automatic stay of removal. No filing fee for I-765 (EAD application). Applicants are waitlisted if the annual cap is reached, ordered by filing date.
Path to permanent residence
T: T-1 holders may adjust to LPR status after 3 years (or end of investigation/prosecution, whichever is earlier). | U: U-1 holders may adjust after 3 years. Both paths lead to potential citizenship.
Key clinical note for IJC students
For T-visa cases: document the trafficking experience thoroughly; law enforcement cooperation is beneficial but not required. For U-visa cases: obtaining the LEA certification (I-918B) is frequently the longest and hardest step — know which agencies in your jurisdiction sign certifications and which don't.
MASSACHUSETTS LAW — ANTI-TRAFFICKING FRAMEWORK
MGL c. 265 §50 — Sex trafficking
Trafficking for sexual servitude: 5–20 years state prison; fines up to $25,000. UP TO LIFE if victim is under 18. Enacted by 2011 'Act Relative to the Commercial Exploitation of People.'
MGL c. 265 §51 — Labor trafficking
Trafficking for forced labor: same penalties as §50. UP TO LIFE if victim is under 18.
MGL c. 265 §26D — Electronic enticement
Electronic enticement of a child into trafficking or commercial sex acts. Separate criminal provision from the core trafficking statutes.
MA Safe Harbor — MGL c. 119 §21
MA law treats commercially sexually exploited minors as VICTIMS OF CHILD ABUSE — not offenders. All minor-trafficking cases must be referred via a 51A child-abuse report to DCF (Department of Children and Families) and handled through a multidisciplinary team (MDT). '51A' = the DCF form number; filing a 51A report is the mandatory mechanism. 'Sexually exploited child' defined in c. 119 §21.
RESULT App (2024)
Recognize & Evaluate Signs to Uncover Labor Trafficking — developed by the MA AG Human Trafficking Division with BU Law and BU Spark!. Free tool for service providers and community members to identify signs of labor trafficking.
MA mandatory reporter obligations
Attorneys are generally NOT mandated reporters for adult trafficking under MA law. For child abuse (including child trafficking), DCF 51A reporting applies to enumerated professionals under MGL c. 119. Know your jurisdiction and practice area.

HUMAN TRAFFICKING PREVENTION MONTH — DATES, THEMES, AND ANCHORS
Presidential observance — January
President Obama proclaimed January as National Slavery and Human Trafficking Prevention Month in January 2010. Reaffirmed annually since. The month has bipartisan recognition and HHS/DHS joint programming.

How the hotline works in practice
Anyone may contact the hotline — survivors, family members, community members, professionals, anonymous tipsters. Trained specialists triage every contact and connect callers to local services, safety planning, and crisis intervention. Data collection is secondary to support — individuals are never questioned solely for data purposes.
TRAUMA-INFORMED LAWYERING — ESSENTIALS FOR LAW STUDENTS
Trauma causes inconsistent statements
Trauma — especially repeated, chronic trauma — impairs memory consolidation and retrieval. Survivors may give different accounts at different times not because they are lying but because trauma physically disrupts memory. A juror or judge who doesn't understand this may incorrectly read inconsistency as deception. Defense attorneys, immigration lawyers, and prosecutors all need this framework.
Coercive control and trauma bonding
Traffickers control survivors through psychological means — debt bondage, threats, emotional manipulation, isolation, document confiscation, and manufactured dependency — not only physical violence. Trauma bonding (a psychological response to intermittent abuse and reward) can make survivors appear to defend their trafficker. Lawyers who do not understand this may misread survivor behavior as lack of victimization.
Client-centered interviewing
Use open-ended, non-leading questions; go at the client's pace; do not assume the client uses the word 'trafficking'; do not push for disclosure. The ABA Child Law Practice guide (Kraemer & Patten) and the Human Trafficking Institute's 'Trauma-Informed Lawyering: Prioritizing the Survivor' are the standard references.
Vacatur and record relief — critical for trafficking survivors
Many trafficking survivors have criminal records for prostitution, drug offenses, or other acts they were forced to commit. Freedom Network USA's Survivor Reentry Project (SRP) is the only national program providing post-conviction criminal record relief (vacatur, expungement, sealing) for trafficking survivors via a nationwide pro bono attorney network — a concrete pro bono pathway for law students. GBLS in Boston also runs a Trafficking Survivor Conviction Relief Initiative.
Restorative justice options
For some survivors, restorative-justice processes (mediation, victim-offender dialogue, community accountability circles) may be preferable to or complementary to retributive prosecution. Lawyers should know these options and discuss them with clients.
Red flags across practice areas
Immigration: clients lacking control of own documents; coached answers; debts owed to a recruiter; fear of law enforcement. / Family law: intimate-partner or familial trafficking; coercive control; parent-child trafficking. / Criminal defense: charges arising from acts the client was forced to commit — vacatur/record-relief tools apply. / Employment: wage theft; confiscated pay; threats tied to immigration status; excessive hours; no freedom of movement. / Housing: instability and homelessness as both vulnerability factor and post-trafficking aftermath. / Healthcare: multiple injuries at different healing stages; accompany a third party to medical appointments; fear of discussing the client's situation.
