Why this campaign

Northeastern Law's Civil Rights and Restorative Justice Project has 1,000+ documented racial homicide case files from the Jim Crow South. Prof. Margaret Burnham sits on the federal Civil Rights Cold Case Records Review Board. The CRRJ launched a Federal Reparations Project in February 2025. NUSL is not observing Black History Month from a distance — it is doing the work.

For FlexJD students in Honolulu and nationwide: every resource here is accessible by phone, email, or web. Every career pathway listed hires from every geography. Every organization listed has remote programming.

This is the through-line for the campaign: the law of race in America, from the Supreme Court's declaration that Black people have 'no rights which the white man was bound to respect' to its 2026 ruling gutting the last meaningful tool for challenging racial vote dilution. Future lawyers need to hold this entire arc in their heads.

By the numbers

Statistics
Black Americans are incarcerated in state prisons at nearly 5x the white rate. 1 in 81 Black adults is in state prison.
The Sentencing Project, 'The Color of Justice' (most recent edition). sentencingproject.org.
Jail incarceration rate: Black 552 per 100,000 vs. white 155 per 100,000 — 3.6x the rate.
Bureau of Justice Statistics, midyear 2023. bjs.gov.
Black males receive sentences approximately 4.7% longer than white males for similar federal offenses and are 23.4% less likely to receive probation.
U.S. Sentencing Commission, 2023. ussc.gov.
Black youth are 5.6x as likely as white youth to be held in juvenile facilities — an all-time high disparity.
The Sentencing Project, 'Black Disparities in Youth Incarceration.' sentencingproject.org.

The legal framework

LANDMARK LEGAL HISTORY — BLACK AMERICA AND THE LAW
1857 — Dred Scott v. Sandford
Chief Justice Taney: enslaved and free Black people are not citizens and have 'no rights which the white man was bound to respect.' Catalyzed the Civil War. Repudiated by the Reconstruction Amendments.
1865 — 13th Amendment
Abolished slavery 'except as a punishment for crime' — the exception connecting slavery to mass incarceration today and a live constitutional argument in prison-labor cases.
1868 — 14th Amendment
Citizenship; Equal Protection; Due Process. The constitutional engine of virtually all civil rights litigation for the next 158 years — and counting.
1870 — 15th Amendment
Prohibits denial of the right to vote based on race, color, or previous condition of servitude. The constitutional basis of the Voting Rights Act and the subject of Callais.
1896 — Plessy v. Ferguson
Upheld 'separate but equal.' Justice Harlan's lone dissent — 'Our Constitution is color-blind' — is now invoked by both sides. Controlled law for 58 years.
1909 — NAACP founded
The National Association for the Advancement of Colored People was co-founded by Ida B. Wells, W.E.B. Du Bois, Moorfield Storey, and others after the 1908 Springfield, IL race riot. From the beginning, its strategy was legal.
1925 — National Bar Association founded
August 1, 1925, Des Moines, Iowa — 12 Black lawyers including Gertrude E. Rush (the only woman co-founder), George H. Woodson, S. Joe Brown, James B. Morris, and Charles P. Howard Sr. — founded the NBA after being denied ABA membership. Rush became the first Black woman licensed to practice law in Iowa.
1938 — Missouri ex rel. Gaines v. Canada
First SCOTUS win for the 'equal in equal protection' litigation strategy developed by Charles Hamilton Houston — requiring Missouri to admit Lloyd Gaines to its law school or build a separate equal facility.
1954/1955 — Brown v. Board of Education I & II
Overruled Plessy in public education: 'separate educational facilities are inherently unequal.' Argued by Thurgood Marshall. Brown II ordered desegregation 'with all deliberate speed' — which is still being litigated in some districts. The single most significant Supreme Court decision of the 20th century.
1964 — Civil Rights Act
Title VI: no discrimination in federally funded programs. Title VII: no discrimination in employment based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. Still the operative federal employment discrimination statute.
1965 — Voting Rights Act
Section 2 (nationwide ban on discriminatory voting practices) and Section 5 (preclearance for covered jurisdictions). Passed after Bloody Sunday, March 7, 1965, Selma, Alabama. The subject of 60 years of litigation and three Supreme Court decisions that have progressively gutted it.
1968 — Fair Housing Act
Passed one week after Dr. King's assassination. See the April campaign page for full treatment.
1971 — Griggs v. Duke Power Co.
Unanimous SCOTUS: Title VII prohibits employer practices that are 'fair in form, but discriminatory in operation' — the disparate-impact doctrine. Required business necessity for facially neutral standards that disproportionately screen out Black workers. Later narrowed by Wards Cove (1989) and codified in the 1991 CRA.
1987 — McCleskey v. Kemp
Rejected the Baldus Study's statistical evidence that Black defendants were significantly more likely to receive the death penalty for killing white victims. The majority held that statistical disparity is insufficient; a defendant must show intentional discrimination in their individual case. Widely criticized as one of the Court's worst modern decisions. Still controls capital punishment jurisprudence.
1993 — Shaw v. Reno
First racial gerrymandering challenge: allowed Equal Protection claims against majority-minority districts whose shape suggests racial considerations predominated. The doctrinal seed that eventually grew into Callais.
2003 — Grutter v. Bollinger
5-4: upheld holistic law-school admissions using race as one factor; Justice O'Connor predicted race-conscious admissions would be unnecessary in 25 years. That timeline was cut short.
2013 — Shelby County v. Holder (570 U.S. 529)
5-4, Roberts: Struck Section 4(b)'s coverage formula, rendering Section 5 preclearance inoperable. Ginsburg dissent: 'Throwing away your umbrella in a rainstorm because you are not getting wet.' Congress has not enacted a new formula.
2021 — Brnovich v. DNC
6-3: Narrowed the framework for Section 2 vote-denial claims. Kagan dissent: 'What is tragic here is that the Court has (yet again) rewritten — in order to weaken — a statute that stands as a monument to America's greatness.'
2023 — SFFA v. Harvard (600 U.S. 181)
6-2/6-3, Roberts: Ended race-conscious admissions. Harvard's Black first-year share: 18% (Class of 2027) → 14% (Class of 2028) → 11.5% (Class of 2029). At 29 elite institutions reporting fall 2025 data, only two still had Black enrollment ≥10%; 11 reported ≤5%.
2025 — Ames v. Ohio (June 5, 2025)
9-0, Jackson, J.: Majority-group ('reverse discrimination') plaintiffs face no heightened 'background circumstances' standard under Title VII. Expected to fuel challenges to corporate DEI programs. Thomas concurrence (joined by Gorsuch) would go further.
★ 2026 — Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026)
6-3, Alito, J.: Struck down Louisiana's two majority-Black congressional districts. LDF: 'gutted Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act in all but name.' Key holdings: (1) illustrative maps must satisfy ALL of a state's political goals; (2) plaintiffs must control for partisanship; (3) 'strong evidence of present-day intentional racial discrimination' required. Cascade: Alabama's discriminatory map restored June 2, 2026; Tennessee and Florida redrew maps within days. Justice Kagan dissent: Section 2 is 'all but a dead letter.' Justice Jackson dissented from the expedited-judgment order, accusing the majority of 'diving into the fray' of active elections.
VOTING RIGHTS — THE ARC COMPLETES
Louisiana v. Callais (April 29, 2026)
See Section 2 for full holding. The single most legally significant BHM development for February 2027. LDF vowed to 'demand new legislation.' The ACLU, Brennan Center, and Lawyers' Committee all condemned the ruling and are pivoting to state courts and legislation.
Allen v. Milligan — post-Callais remand (2026)
On May 11, 2026 SCOTUS vacated the district court's 2025 intentional-discrimination finding (which had invalidated Alabama's 2023 map) and remanded in light of Callais. On May 26, 2026 the district court reinstated its injunction; SCOTUS stayed that on June 2, 2026 (Sotomayor dissenting, joined by Kagan and Jackson) — restoring Alabama's 2023 plan for the 2026 elections.
John R. Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act (2025)
Reintroduced in the House by Rep. Terri Sewell (D-AL) on March 5, 2025 (the 60th anniversary of Bloody Sunday) as H.R. 14; in the Senate by Sens. Durbin and Warnock on July 29, 2025. Would restore preclearance and respond to Shelby and Brnovich. Status: pending in committee; no Republican support; unlikely to pass the current Congress.
What remains after Shelby + Brnovich + Callais
Section 2 still exists on paper for intentional-discrimination claims. The 14th and 15th Amendments remain. State constitutions and state courts are now the principal battleground for voting rights protection in jurisdictions without federal preclearance.
DEI ROLLBACK AND AFFIRMATIVE ACTION
SFFA v. Harvard (2023) aftermath
Harvard Black first-year share: 18% (Class of 2027) → 14% (Class of 2028) → 11.5% (Class of 2029). Princeton 9%→5%; Amherst 11%→6%. Of 29 elite institutions reporting fall 2025 data, only 2 had Black enrollment ≥10%; 11 reported ≤5%. ABA data shows law-school racial diversity did not decline in 2024.
Executive Orders 14151 + 14173 (January 20–21, 2025)
EO 14151: ended federal DEI programs. EO 14173: revoked EO 11246 (affirmative action for federal contractors, the 1965 LBJ order). DOE Feb. 14, 2025 'Dear Colleague' letter permanently vacated. EOs targeted law firms by name; EEOC sent information demands to 20 firms. March 2026 EO extended anti-DEI certification requirements to federal contractors.
Ames v. Ohio (June 5, 2025) — unanimous
9-0, Jackson, J.: Majority-group plaintiffs face no heightened standard under Title VII. Expected to fuel reverse-discrimination suits challenging DEI. Thomas concurrence would go further.
HBCU funding (September 2025)
Trump redirected ~$435M to HBCUs on Sept. 15, 2025 (bringing FY2025 HBCU funding above $1.34 billion, +48.4% YoY) — while simultaneously cutting ~$350M for other minority-serving institutions.
CRIMINAL JUSTICE AND REPARATIONS
H.R. 40 — Reparations Commission (reintroduced 2025)
Reintroduced by Rep. Pressley (D-MA) and Sen. Booker (D-NJ) on January 3, 2025 (formally announced February 12, 2025, BHM). Would create a federal commission to study slavery's legacies and recommend remedies including a formal apology and compensation. ~99 Democratic co-sponsors; referred to Judiciary; not advancing in current Congress.
CRRJ Federal Reparations Project — NUSL
The Civil Rights and Restorative Justice Project launched a Federal Reparations: Lynching and Racial Violence Project at a February 27–28, 2025 conference ('Towards Justice') at Mills College at Northeastern. NUSL is not just watching — it is doing the reparations research.
First Step Act (2018) — implementation
Remains in force. 18,084 people were released from federal custody in calendar year 2024 after applying FSA time credits (U.S. Sentencing Commission, updated March 16, 2026). BOP Director Marshall directed 'full implementation' on June 17, 2025.
George Floyd Justice in Policing Act
Reintroduced H.R. 5361 (September 2025): bans chokeholds and no-knock warrants in drug cases, ends qualified immunity for officers, creates a National Police Misconduct Registry, curbs racial profiling. Not enacted.
McCleskey v. Kemp (1987) — still controlling
Still the controlling standard for statistical challenges to racial disparity in capital sentencing: a defendant must show intentional discrimination in their individual case, not merely statistical patterns. The Baldus Study's finding — that Black defendants were 4.3x more likely to receive the death penalty for killing white victims — was insufficient.

Key facts & framing

BLACK LAWYERS, JUDGES, AND ADVOCATES EVERY LAW STUDENT SHOULD KNOW
Charles Hamilton Houston (1895–1950)
Howard Law dean and 'the man who killed Jim Crow.' He designed the NAACP's legal strategy that dismantled Plessy case by case — from Missouri ex rel. Gaines (1938) to Sweatt v. Painter to Shelley v. Kraemer. He died five years before Brown was decided, but Brown was his plan. Thurgood Marshall called him 'the Moses of that journey.'
Thurgood Marshall (1908–1993)
Argued 32 cases before the U.S. Supreme Court and won 29 — including Brown v. Board of Education. First Black U.S. Solicitor General (1965–1967). First Black Supreme Court Justice (1967–1991). Founded the NAACP Legal Defense and Educational Fund.
Constance Baker Motley (1921–2005)
First Black woman appointed to the federal bench (S.D.N.Y., 1966). First Black woman to argue before the U.S. Supreme Court (1961). As an LDF litigator, argued 10 cases before SCOTUS and won 9, including desegregation cases in Alabama, Mississippi, and Georgia. Represented Martin Luther King Jr.
Jane Bolin (1908–2007)
First Black female judge in the United States — appointed to the New York City Domestic Relations Court in 1939 by Mayor LaGuardia. First Black woman to graduate from Yale Law School (1931). Served on the court for 40 years.
Show 7 more items
Gertrude E. Rush (1880–1962)
Co-founder and first president of the National Bar Association (1925). First Black woman licensed to practice law in Iowa (1918). The NBA's Gertrude Rush Award is named in her honor.
Kimberlé Crenshaw (b. 1959)
Columbia/UCLA law professor who coined 'intersectionality' (1989) — the framework for understanding how overlapping identities (race, gender, class) compound discrimination. Co-founder of the African American Policy Forum (AAPF); creator of #SayHerName. Her intersectionality framework is now cited in legal decisions worldwide.
Bryan Stevenson (b. 1959)
Founder and Executive Director of the Equal Justice Initiative (EJI, 1989). Author of Just Mercy (2014). Argued before the U.S. Supreme Court. Received the American Bar Association's highest honor (2018 Thurgood Marshall Award). Created the National Memorial for Peace and Justice and the Legacy Museum (both 2018, Montgomery, AL) and the Freedom Monument Sculpture Park (March 27, 2024).
Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson (b. 1970)
First Black woman to serve on the U.S. Supreme Court (confirmed April 7, 2022). Harvard AB 1992, Harvard Law 1996. Former Public Defender. As of June 2026, the Court's most frequent dissenter — including solo dissents in Trump v. CASA (birthright-citizenship injunctions), Chiles v. Salazar (conversion therapy), and a separate dissent from the Callais expedited-judgment order.
Janai Nelson
8th President and Director-Counsel of the NAACP Legal Defense Fund (since March 2022). First Black woman to hold the title President. Former Associate Director-Counsel at LDF. In response to Callais: 'We will demand new legislation.' LDF is the principal litigant in the Milligan and Callais redistricting cases.
Sherrilyn Ifill
