Why this campaign

NUSL describes itself as 'the queerest law school in the nation.' This is not hyperbole — it is institutional identity, and it is the authentic anchor for this campaign.

In fall 2022, 30% of incoming JD and FlexJD students self-identified as LGBTQ+ (NUSL, Nov. 2, 2022). That is nearly one in three law students entering NUSL. The Queer Caucus (QC) is one of the most active student organizations at the school. Alumni have shaped landmark LGBTQ+ legal history. And in December 2024, a Northeastern Law alumnus became the first openly transgender lawyer to argue a case before the United States Supreme Court.

VIOLENCE AGAINST TRANS PEOPLE — TDOR 2025 DATA
27
trans and gender-nonconforming people were killed in the US in the year leading up to TDOR 2025 (Nov. 2024–Nov. 20, 2025)
HRC Foundation, 'Remembrance Is Not Enough,' released Nov. 20, 2025. hrc.org. HRC notes 'at least' — many deaths go unreported or misgendered by law enforcement and media.
399
trans and gender-nonconforming people killed in the US since HRC began tracking in 2013
HRC 2025 TDOR Report. Of the 399: 70% were Black people and nearly 60% were Black trans women. (HRC uses this specific breakdown rather than a single "people of color" figure.)
A4TE 2025 Remembrance Report: 58 trans people lost since TDOR 2024 — 27 by violence, 21 by suicide, 8 natural causes
Advocates for Trans Equality (A4TE), transequality.org. Released Nov. 13, 2025. A4TE counts all causes of loss; HRC counts fatal violence only. Both numbers are correct and serve different purposes.
TGEU: 281 trans people murdered worldwide Oct. 2024–Sept. 2025; 88% trans people of color
Trans Europe and Central Asia (TGEU) Trans Murder Monitoring, tgeu.org. Global figure.
VIOLENCE, VICTIMIZATION AND POVERTY — BROADER DATA
LGBT people experience 106.4 violent victimizations per 1,000 persons; trans people experience 93.7 per 1,000 — compared to 21.1 per 1,000 among non-LGBT people
Williams Institute, UCLA School of Law, pooled 2022–2023 National Crime Victimization Survey (NCVS). williamsinstitute.law.ucla.edu.
LGBT people experience violent hate crimes at 6.4 per 1,000, compared to 0.7 per 1,000 among non-LGBT people — approximately 9 times the rate
Williams Institute, same pooled 2022–2023 NCVS source above.
Trans people experience violent victimization at 86.2 per 1,000 vs. 21.7 per 1,000 for cisgender people — more than four times as often. 1 in 4 trans women who were victimized believed the incident was motivated by anti-trans bias.
Williams Institute, American Journal of Public Health, March 2021, pooled 2017–2018 NCVS. Older data but widely cited and peer-reviewed.

NEU CHOSEN NAME + GENDER MARKER POLICY — WHAT STUDENTS NEED TO KNOW
Class rosters, Canvas, Outlook, Husky ID card
Chosen name displays in lieu of legal name in all systems that can accommodate it, including class rosters, transcripts, Canvas, Outlook contact card, and the Husky ID card. Process: submit a Personal Details Update ServiceNow ticket (students). Updates can take several weeks.
Diploma
Students may choose to have EITHER the chosen name or the legal name printed on the diploma. Specify your preference in the ServiceNow ticket.
Gender marker in university systems
Gender markers display in the housing and intramural-sports systems. Process: same ServiceNow ticket. Gender marker ≠ legal sex — updating it in NU systems does not update legal documents.
Documents requiring legal name
Financial aid, billing, immigration documents, and tax documents require the legal name regardless of chosen-name preferences. This is federal/legal compliance, not university preference.

The legal framework

MASSACHUSETTS — CURRENT LEGAL FRAMEWORK FOR TRANS PEOPLE
✅ MAJOR 2025 UPDATE: MA name-change law (eff. Nov. 25, 2025)
Gov. Healey signed a budget provision on Nov. 25, 2025 ELIMINATING the newspaper publication requirement for legal name changes and making name-change court records CONFIDENTIAL, effective immediately. (Source: GBH/WGBH Nov. 26, 2025; GLAD's Polly Crozier.) Approx. 6,200 name-change petitions filed annually in MA. This supersedes ALL prior guidance about publication requirements.
Legal name change — Process (Probate and Family Court)
File petition in Probate and Family Court in your MA county. Filing fee: ~$150–$500; fee waivers available via Affidavit of Indigency. Records now confidential per Nov. 2025 law. MTPC Namesake platform auto-fills the paperwork (free). Resources: masstpc.org/what-we-do/ida-network, GLAD Law gladlaw.org/issues/gender-x-marker-massachusetts
Birth certificate gender-marker amendment (MA)
Registry of Vital Records & Statistics | 150 Mt. Vernon St., 1st Floor, Dorchester MA 02125 | main: 617-740-2600 | amendments: 617-740-2674 / NO medical documentation or court order required — self-attestation only. X (non-binary) marker available. Separate from legal name change.
Driver's license / ID gender marker (RMV)
Self-attestation; no documentation required. X marker available. mass.gov/how-to/change-gender-marker-on-your-massachusetts-license-or-id
Trump v. Orr (Nov. 6, 2025) — Federal passports
607 U.S. ___ (2025). SCOTUS stayed the district court injunction, allowing the State Department to enforce its policy of issuing passports reflecting sex assigned at birth. Justice Jackson dissented (joined by Sotomayor and Kagan). Over 214,000 public comments had opposed the policy. State-level documents (birth certificate, driver's license) are UNAFFECTED — this applies only to federal documents (passports, federal ID).
MA Shield Act 2.0 (St. 2025 c. 16) — Signed Aug. 7, 2025
Gov. Healey called it 'the strongest law in America that will protect reproductive health care, gender-affirming care, and those who provide it.' / Key provisions: blocks MA courts from treating a parent's support of a child's transgender care as abuse or neglect; prescription labels may display practice name instead of individual physician (safety protection for providers); codifies emergency abortion access; limits release of sensitive health data to out-of-state authorities. / MA is one of 22 states plus D.C. with shield protections for trans healthcare.
MA Gender Identity Nondiscrimination Law (MGL c. 272 §98, St. 2016 c. 134)
Prohibits discrimination based on gender identity in public accommodations including restrooms and locker rooms. Still in force. Affirms the right of trans people to use facilities consistent with gender identity.
MA Employment and Housing Nondiscrimination (MGL c. 151B)
Prohibits gender identity discrimination in employment, housing, credit, education, and public accommodations. Enforced by the MA Commission Against Discrimination (MCAD): 617-994-6000 | mass.gov/mcad
Additional MA protective statutes
MGL c. 12 §11I½ — Interference with gender-affirming care is a civil rights violation / St. 2025 c. 16 — Shield Act 2.0 (see above) / MGL c. 112 §275 — Conversion therapy banned for people under 18 / MGL c. 111 §72GG — Long-term-care nondiscrimination (eff. June 3, 2025) / MGL c. 272 §98 — Public accommodations (gender identity protected since 2016)
THE 2025–2026 FEDERAL LEGAL LANDSCAPE
★ United States v. Skrmetti (SCOTUS, June 18, 2025) — MOST IMPORTANT 2025 CASE
6-3 decision. Chief Justice Roberts wrote for the majority; Thomas and Barrett concurring; Alito concurring in part; Sotomayor dissented (joined by Jackson; Kagan in part). / Holding: Tennessee SB1's ban on puberty blockers and hormones for transgender minors SURVIVES rational-basis review. The law classifies only on age and medical use — not on sex or transgender status — so it does not trigger heightened scrutiny. / Notably, the Court expressly declined to extend Bostock v. Clayton County beyond Title VII. Bostock remains the law for Title VII employment discrimination but was NOT expanded. / Also: 27 state gender-affirming care bans for minors now in effect; Montana and Arkansas bans remain blocked pending further proceedings. / Chase Strangio '10 argued for the plaintiffs.
Bostock v. Clayton County (SCOTUS, 2020) — Still good law
Title VII of the Civil Rights Act prohibits discrimination against employees based on sexual orientation and gender identity. Employers cannot fire, demote, or harass workers who are gay or transgender. Still the controlling federal employment discrimination standard. Skrmetti expressly declined to extend it beyond Title VII but did NOT disturb it.
Trump Executive Order 14168 (Jan. 20, 2025)
'Defending Women from Gender Ideology Extremism' — directs federal agencies to recognize only two sexes defined at conception; requires passports, visas, and Global Entry to reflect sex assigned at birth. Trump v. Orr (Nov. 6, 2025) allowed enforcement while litigation continues.
Trump Executive Order 14183 (Jan. 27, 2025)
'Prioritizing Military Excellence and Readiness' — trans military ban. SCOTUS allowed enforcement May 6, 2025. D.C. Circuit ruled June 1, 2026: currently serving trans members may continue, but the military services may refuse new trans enlistees.
Trump Executive Order 14187 (Jan. 28, 2025)
Seeks to ban federally funded gender-affirming care for people under 19. Blocked by preliminary injunction in PFLAG v. Trump (March 2025) — as of June 2026, the ban is NOT in effect but litigation continues.
EEOC and federal agency rollbacks
HUD proposed rolling back trans protections in federally funded shelters under the Equal Access Rule. EEOC removed gender-identity references from agency materials. Federal enforcement posture has shifted even though Bostock remains the statutory law.
Trump v. Orr (Nov. 6, 2025)
SCOTUS stayed the district court injunction in the passport case. State Department is enforcing sex-assigned-at-birth on passports. State-level IDs unaffected. Over 214,000 public comments opposed. Justice Jackson dissented.
KEY CASES FOR LAW SCHOOL DISCUSSION
Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins (SCOTUS, 1989)
Established sex-stereotyping doctrine under Title VII: employers cannot discriminate based on failure to conform to gender norms. The doctrinal foundation that later supported Bostock.
Grimm v. Gloucester County School Board (4th Cir., 2020)
Title IX protects transgender students. The 4th Circuit ruled that Gavin Grimm had the right to use the bathroom consistent with his gender identity. SCOTUS declined cert.
Whitaker v. Kenosha Unified School District (7th Cir., 2017)
Title IX and Equal Protection both protect trans students' right to use restrooms consistent with gender identity. Another circuit precedent.
Kadel v. Folwell (4th Cir., 2024 — pre-Skrmetti)
4th Circuit struck down Medicaid exclusions for gender-affirming care under intermediate scrutiny. Vacated and remanded in light of Skrmetti; outcome on remand pending.
Shilling v. Trump (D.D.C., pending)
Lambda Legal and HRC challenging the trans military ban under First and Fifth Amendments. Active litigation as of June 2026.

Key facts & framing

TDOR-SPECIFIC FRAMING GUIDANCE
TDOR is a solemn grief and memorial observance — not a celebration
Tone should reflect grief, witness, and resolve — not awareness-month uplift. The reading of names is a ritual of mourning. Content should be dignified, not sensationalized. Silence and solemnity are appropriate on Nov. 20. Trans Awareness Week (Nov. 13–19) is the appropriate window for educational and resource-focused content.
Use chosen name and correct pronouns for each victim
GLAAD guidance: use each person's CHOSEN name and correct pronouns — which may differ from what appeared in law enforcement reports or media. This honors their identity and counters the common pattern of posthumous misgendering.
Do NOT sensationalize violence
GLAAD: avoid graphic descriptions of how individuals were killed. State the fact that they were murdered; do not reproduce violent imagery or details. This is consistent with AFSP Safe Messaging Guidelines and GLAAD's Media Reference Guide.
Pair memorial content with living trans people's contributions
GLAAD: the exclusive focus on death and violence can dehumanize trans people and distort the public perception of trans lives. Balance TDOR memorial content with the diversity, resilience, creativity, and legal advocacy of the living trans community. Highlight Chase Strangio '10, Prof. Adler, trans-led organizations like Black and Pink MA.
AFSP Safe Messaging Guidelines — always in effect
Safe messaging applies even at TDOR, where the deaths involve violence, not suicide. Key rules: always include crisis resources (988, Trans Lifeline); do not describe the method or means of any individual killing; use 'died' or 'was killed,' not sensationalized language. See afsp.org/safe-messaging
Trans Awareness Week content — educational and resource focus
Nov. 13–19: lead with the legal landscape (Skrmetti, Shield Act 2.0, executive orders), MA resources, NUSL's LGBTQ+ identity, Digital Transgender Archive, Prof. Adler's work, and the Queer Caucus. Profiles of living trans lawyers and advocates. Statistics that contextualize why legal protection matters.
Virtual access for FlexJD students
For FlexJD students in Honolulu and across the US who cannot attend the Boston ISG Museum coalition vigil: promote the 'Give Them Their Flowers' national virtual TDOR vigil (GLAAD + A4TE + Transgender Law Center + HRC + Equality Federation + Keshet, Nov. 20 annually). Check glaad.org/tdor for the 2026 registration link. This is the remote participation option.

Additional resources

November 28, 1998 — Rita Hester's murder
Rita Hester, a Black transgender woman, was murdered in her apartment in Allston, Massachusetts. Her case received minimal media attention and was initially reported with dehumanizing language misgendering her. Her death became the catalyst for TDOR.
1999 — TDOR founded
Gwendolyn Ann Smith, a transgender advocate and graphic designer, organized the first Transgender Day of Remembrance in 1999 to memorialize Rita Hester. Co-founders included Nancy Nangeroni and Jahaira DeAlto. The first vigils were held in BOSTON and San Francisco.
Other Massachusetts victims honored in the founding era
Chanelle Pickett — murdered in Watertown, MA, 1995. Monique Thomas — murdered in Dorchester, MA, 1998. Both Black trans women; both cases illustrate the pattern of anti-trans violence concentrated among Black trans women that TDOR continues to address.
Academic framing (for law school audiences)
Scholars including Sadie Lamble (2008) and C. Riley Snorton and Jin Haritaworn have argued that TDOR vigils can inadvertently center white spectatorship rather than the trans women of color most affected by fatal violence. This critique is consistent with foregrounding organizations like Black and Pink MA, centering Black trans–led voices in your TDOR content, and GLAAD's guidance to pair memorial content with living trans people's contributions. Useful nuance for a law school audience that values critical engagement.
November 20 — fixed annual date
TDOR is always November 20. Trans Awareness Week is always November 13–19. GLAAD frames the full observance as approximately November 12–20. The Boston coalition vigil date and location (2025: ISG Museum, Nov. 20, 7:30 PM) should be confirmed with MTPC each fall for the current year.
