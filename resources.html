Program Overview

What is the FlexJD?
The FlexJD is NUSL's ABA-accredited, part-time, primarily online Juris Doctor — the only part-time JD at NUSL. Designed for working professionals, it delivers the same 83-credit JD over four years through live online classes, asynchronous coursework, and six required in-person intensives (LOCs) in Boston. The diploma is identical to the full-time JD. Program page: law.northeastern.edu/admissions/flexjd

Disability Access Services (DAS)

You don't have to navigate this alone — and you don't have to know exactly what you need before reaching out.

Northeastern is required to provide reasonable accommodations for qualified students with disabilities — including instructional, course-load, or exam modifications, interpreters, note-takers, and co-op accommodations. This applies fully to FlexJD students, remote or on-ground. Note: the office was renamed from the Disability Resource Center (DRC) to Disability Access Services (DAS) in July 2024 — if your handbook still says DRC, that's outdated language; DAS is the current name.

StepWhat to do
1Contact the Office of Academic and Student Affairs (ASA) to discuss next steps — you can do this confidentially before deciding whether to formally disclose.
2ASA will refer you to Disability Access Services (DAS) for screening and assessment.
3DAS provides these services free of charge.

Timing matters: if you want accommodations in place for the current semester, reach out as early as possible — processing takes time, and cutoffs exist for each term.

Co-op note: accommodations on co-op work differently than in-classroom accommodations, since you hold both student and employee status simultaneously. Ask ASA about this specifically if you're heading into a co-op term.

Deficiency System

Academic Probation: Triggered at 2 cumulative deficiencies. Lasts 2 academic semesters. Restrictions: no dual degrees/certificates; must take Professional Responsibility, Evidence, 2 additional bar-related courses, and LAW 7652 in final term; all enrollments require Associate Dean approval.

Dismissal triggers (any one): (1) 6+ deficiencies during first year; (2) Fail in any 1L course; (3) 2+ Marginal Passes on retaken 1L exams/courses; (4) 2 deficiencies in any upper-level term while on probation; (5) failing a 1L course after a third retake; (6) failure to complete 2 cooperative work terms.

Grade appeals: 4-step process (discuss with instructor, prepare a written appeal statement within 28 days, college-level appeal, then university-level appeal to the Academic Appeals Resolution Committee if unresolved within 42 days); grades change only for clerical errors. Source: catalog.northeastern.edu/graduate/academic-policies-procedures/appeals

Satisfactory Academic Progress (SAP) for federal financial aid: No GPA-based threshold at NUSL — good academic standing under the Handbook operationalizes the qualitative SAP standard. The 67% completion-of-attempted-credits and 150% maximum-timeframe quantitative rules apply normally. SAP appeals: studentfinance.northeastern.edu/policies-procedures/satisfactory-academic-progress

Enrollment, Co-op & Requirement Details (per ASA)

Per-semester upper-level load: minimum 7 credits, maximum 10. Enrolling under 7 or at 11 requires a petition to ASA (under 7 only granted in extraordinary circumstances: Under Enrollment Notification Form; 11 credits: Over Enrollment Form). 12+ credits is not permitted. Separately, at least 67 of your 83 total credits must come from courses with direct faculty instruction (including online coursework) — petition ASA to waive this down to 64 if needed.

Co-op, in detail: your one required co-op is 8 credits (6 for the 300-hour field placement + 2 for the concurrent reflections seminar), run at ~20–21 hrs/week over a 14-week semester. It must be supervised legal work, distinct from your regular job duties; co-ops with your current employer are possible but need Co-op Office approval and a conflicts-of-interest review. You cannot take other coursework (besides the reflections seminar) or do TA/RA work during your co-op semester. Arrange it with the Co-op Office (lawcoop@northeastern.edu) and FlexJD Co-op Advisor Hanna Kim; typically done the spring after 1L year, though the pattern can be adjusted with your advisor (requires this form).

The separate practical experience requirement (a second co-op, a field placement, or an approved experiential project — e.g. a professor-run policy project, a practice-focused RA role, or a project through NU’s Experiential Network or Community-Engaged Teaching and Research) needs a minimum 275 hours during a non-academic semester, typically the summer after 3L year, with no coursework or TA/RA work alongside it.

The 1,000-hour work experience requirement can be any relevant professional work (not necessarily legal) completed during your four years in the program, and is waived if you had 3+ years of professional experience before starting FlexJD. Document completion or request the waiver via this form.

The Public Interest Requirement is satisfied by any one of: a co-op in a public interest setting (this alone auto-satisfies it), one of NUSL’s public interest clinics, 30 pre-approved uncompensated hours in a public interest setting (after 1L year), or a 3-credit directed study with faculty approval. Qualifying settings include government agencies, legal aid/public defender offices, victim advocacy organizations, and pro bono/law-reform organizations. It does not include judicial clerkships, work at a private firm (unless exclusively on the firm’s own pro bono matter), or pro bono work through your current employer. Document it via the Public Interest Requirement Completion Form.

The Upper-Level Writing Requirement needs a revised, well-researched piece of at least 15 pages (3,750 words) — or a 2–3 piece portfolio from one course or co-op — certified by a supervising professor (or, for co-op writing, by your supervisor plus Faculty Review Committee review). Submit via the Writing Requirement Completion Form. If you haven’t completed a qualifying piece by your final semester, you must submit a Writing Requirement Plan by the Add Deadline and finish by the last day of exams. You cannot use the same course for both this requirement and the Experiential Education Requirement.

Disclaimer (per ASA): this guidance is provided for planning purposes — always confirm final requirements against the Student Handbook.

Certificates, Concentrations & Dual Degrees

NUSL offers seven JD x Certificates — interdisciplinary certificates built into the JD, combining coursework, co-ops, clinics, or a substantial paper: Health Law and Policy, Human Rights Law, Legal Design, Poverty Law and Economic Justice, Privacy Law, Sustainability and Climate Change, and Women, Gender, Sexuality and the Law. Source: law.northeastern.edu/academics/programs/jd/jdx-certificates. The 2025–26 Academic Catalog lists six of these as stand-alone graduate certificates (all but Sustainability and Climate Change, which appears to be newer than the catalog’s last update) plus separate business-focused certificates in Business Law, Healthcare Compliance, Health Law, Human Resources Law, Intellectual Property Law, and United States Law. Source: catalog.northeastern.edu/graduate/law/graduate-certificates.

NUSL also offers seven dual-degree programs: JD/MSAMBA (Accounting and Business Administration), JD/MBA, JD/PhD in Criminology and Justice Policy, JD/MS in Criminology and Criminal Justice, JD/MPH, JD/MPP, and LLM/MBA. Source: catalog.northeastern.edu/graduate/law/dual-degrees. These are built around full-time, in-person coursework and dedicated co-op semesters (the JD/MBA, for example, includes three full-time co-op terms) — none of the catalog pages confirm a part-time or remote track. Treat dual degrees as presumptively incompatible with the FlexJD schedule unless your advisor confirms otherwise in writing.

The official caveat on every certificate applies directly to FlexJD: “Concentrations and course offerings may vary by campus and/or by program modality... certain options may be required at certain campuses or for certain program modalities.” Confirm current eligibility and course availability with your advisor or admissions coach before counting on any certificate or dual degree.

Clinics & Externships

NUSL runs seven in-house clinics, each a 25-hour-per-week commitment based in Boston: Civil Rights and Restorative Justice, Community Business, Domestic Violence, Immigrant Justice, Intellectual Property Law, Prisoners’ Rights, and Public Health Advocacy. More than 100 students participate annually, contributing over 25,000 hours of legal assistance a year (U.S. News ranks NUSL No. 19 for clinical programs). Source: law.northeastern.edu/experience/clinics. Most of these require weekly in-person presence and are not realistic for a remote schedule — except one: the Virtual Legal Advocacy Clinic (FlexJD), LAW 7983, built specifically for remote FlexJD students and confirmed on NUSL’s own Experiential Education Requirement course list. That’s the clinic option to ask about if you’re not Boston-based.

Contact: Professor Hemanth C. Gundavaram, Director of Clinical Programs (617.373.6802); James Tracey, Administrative Operations Manager, Law Clinics (617.373.8947).

Externships and co-ops are the other remote-friendly path: FlexJD students can access the same co-op employer pool (1,000+ employers across 40 states and 71 countries) for flexible/remote placements. Always verify current placement options with the Co-op and Career office before registering: nulawcareers.sites.northeastern.edu.

Registration & Deadlines

Registration Process

Adding a course: you cannot add anything after the first 14 calendar days of an upper-level semester. Dropping a course: you can drop any upper-level course within the first 21 calendar days of the term with no transcript record; drop after that window and a “W” (withdraw) appears on your transcript instead. For limited-enrollment courses and clinics specifically, tell the instructor by the end of the first class session if you're not staying, or drop before the end of week 3 to avoid the “W.” First-year students need the Dean's advance approval to drop a required 1L course — only granted for compelling reasons. Source: NUSL JD Student Information Handbook (2024–25 edition — confirm current-year edition with ASA), Adding and Dropping Offerings.

FlexJD Tuition & Budgeting

2025–26 FlexJD estimated student expense budget: tuition $49,230, living expenses/transportation $24,000, books & supplies $2,250, student activity fee $51, loan origination fees $198. Living/transportation allowance is capped at $2,000/month ($685/month if living with parents) — contact Financial Aid if your actual costs run higher. Source: law.northeastern.edu/admissions/flexjd/tuition-and-budgeting.

Tuition & Fees

Tuition is distributed evenly across three semesters (fall, spring, summer) in years 1–3, then across two semesters (fall, spring) in year 4. Your $500 tuition deposit applies to your first bill. Health insurance ($2,849 for 2025–26, billed fall term only, waivable with comparable coverage via the StudentHub portal) and a one-time budget increase for the MPRE + one bar exam fee, a computer purchase (up to $2,000), or documented childcare costs can all be added — bring receipts to Financial Aid. A monthly payment plan is available through Nelnet Campus Commerce for a $35/term enrollment fee (no interest): northeastern.afford.com or 855.217.0551.

Tuition Refund Schedule (Withdrawal)

FlexJD first-year students (your first four terms — fall, spring, summer, fall) get 80% back in week 1, 60% in week 2, 40% in week 3, 20% in week 4, 0% after week 4. FlexJD upper-level students (from your fifth term on) get 100% through week 3, 60% in week 4, 40% in week 5, 0% after. Non-attendance does not count as withdrawal — you must formally withdraw. Seat deposits are never refundable. Federal loan funds follow the separate Title IV Return calculation (fully "earned" once you've attended 60% of the term). Source: law.northeastern.edu/admissions/flexjd/tuition-and-budgeting.

Federal Aid

File the FAFSA every year (Northeastern's school code: 002199; priority filing date February 15; no parental info required for law students). For 2025–26: Federal Direct Unsubsidized Stafford Loans go up to $20,500/year (5.28% fixed interest), with a $138,500 career maximum; Federal Grad PLUS Loans cover the gap up to your full cost of attendance (6.28% fixed interest, repayment over 10–25 years).

⚠️ Grad PLUS is being eliminated for periods of instruction starting after June 30, 2026 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act — the Stafford annual cap is rising to $50,000/year (professional students) to partly offset it. If you're already borrowing Grad PLUS for your current program, you can generally keep borrowing at current terms as a continuing student. See the newsletter's Federal Student Loans section for the full breakdown, and confirm your own situation with Financial Aid before assuming anything.

Contact: NUSL Financial Aid Office, lawfinaid@northeastern.edu, 617.373.4620. Source: law.northeastern.edu/admissions/flexjd/financial-aid, studentfinance.northeastern.edu/applying-for-aid/federal-updates.

Leave of Absence

The Dean may approve a leave of absence for up to one year, letting you step away and return without reapplying. To come back, submit a written petition to the Associate Dean for Student Affairs at least 14 calendar days before the semester you want to reenter, explaining the circumstances that led to the leave. The Associate Dean reviews it (consulting other law school/university offices as needed) to confirm you're eligible to continue. Remember: the 7-year completion clock (ABA Standard 311(b)) keeps running regardless of leave time, so factor that in before requesting one. Confirm current procedure with ASA and the JD Student Handbook before filing. Source: registrar.northeastern.edu/article/leaves-of-absence.

FAQ

Can I keep my full-time job?
Yes — designed for working professionals. ~20 hours/week, 1–2 of those possibly during the workday.
Is the diploma the same as the full-time JD?
Yes — identical JD; no FlexJD notation on the diploma.
Can I sit for the bar in any state?
Most states — but not New York or Alabama. Verify your target state at ncbex.org. Contact Antoinette Coakley (617.373.7620).
Are all NUSL courses available to FlexJD students?
No. Despite what the official page states, several courses (including Securities Regulation, Inside Counsel, Information Privacy Law, Negotiations, Whistleblower Law, and M&A) are not opened to FlexJD students. The catalog does not flag this — a known transparency issue being raised with administration. Always confirm with Hanna Kim (617.373.8847).
Is the LSAT required, or is the GRE also accepted?
The FlexJD admissions page requires LSAT (≤5 years old). Third-party sources suggest NUSL accepts GRE for the full-time JD but the FlexJD page does not mention GRE. Confirm at FlexJD@northeastern.edu or 617.203.7228 before assuming GRE is accepted.
Are there scholarships specific to FlexJD students?
Yes — HBCU Scholarship (half to full tuition, March 1 deadline), PILS (full tuition), Health Policy & Law Scholarship (NUSL's highest honor), endowed scholarships each fall via Weekly Wire, co-op stipends, and the Loan Deferral and Forgiveness (LDF) Program for public interest graduates with adjusted net income ≤ $85,000. NUSL awarded over $6.7 million in scholarships and grants to first-year students last year.
What happens if I need to take a semester off?
Petition the Dean for a LOA of up to 1 year without reapplying. Submit a written re-entry petition to Sonia Mee (Associate Dean, 617.373.3811) ≥14 calendar days before return. If your leave exceeds 6 months, federal loan repayment may begin — contact lawfinaid@northeastern.edu before taking any leave.
Can I switch to the full-time program?
Yes, after completing 1L with academic dean approval.
What is the SBA and how does it represent FlexJD students?
The SBA (Student Bar Association) is the elected student representative body affiliated with the ABA Law Student Division. The Chair of FlexJD Interests is a dedicated SBA seat representing all FlexJD cohorts. Whether this role carries a voting seat on the SBA executive board is unconfirmed from public sources — contact sba@northeastern.edu to verify.

Reaching Out to Alumni: A Practical Guide

Alumni outreach is one of the most underused career tools available to law students. Here is how to do it well — starting in 1L year, not after graduation.

What to do
1 — FindGo to LinkedIn. Search NUSL graduates by practice area keyword: mergers and acquisitions, venture capital, entrepreneurship, in-house counsel, securities, transactional law, dispute resolution, M&A, corporate law. Identify 3–5 attorneys whose practice area interests you.
2 — MessageSend a short, specific connection message or InMail. 3–4 sentences: (1) you are a current NUSL FlexJD student; (2) one sentence on why you are reaching out to them specifically; (3) a request for 10 minutes of Zoom time for career advice. Do not ask for a job. Do not ask them to review your resume. Ask for 10 minutes of their time and perspective.
3 — PrepareResearch their career path before the call. Have 3–5 genuine questions ready. Examples: "What would you tell a 1L interested in transactional work that you wish you had known?" / "What skills matter most in your practice area that law school does not teach?"
4 — Follow upSend a thank-you within 24 hours. One short paragraph, specific to what they said. This is how a 10-minute call becomes a long-term professional relationship.
5 — RepeatSet a goal of 2–3 outreach messages per month starting in 1L year. The compound effect over three years — conducted respectfully and specifically — is significant.

This self-directed approach pairs well with NUSL's own alumni infrastructure — the Office of Development and Alumni Relations runs a Connections program that can help match students with alumni by practice area and location. Use both: the institutional program surfaces contacts you might not find on your own, and the LinkedIn approach above gives you a direct way to build the relationship once you have a name.
