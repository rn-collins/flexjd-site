Glossary

FlexJD & legal terms, in plain English

Two lists in one page: the program-specific shorthand you'll hear from day one (LOC, ASA, LSSC, deficiency), and the general legal vocabulary that shows up in class before anyone stops to define it. Search both at once.

FlexJD & NUSL Program TermsGeneral Legal Vocabulary
01

FlexJD & NUSL Program Terms

LOC (Law On Campus)
One of six mandatory in-person residencies (~4 days each) at NUSL's Boston campus, required across the four-year FlexJD program even though the degree is otherwise remote/asynchronous.
ASA (Office of Academic and Student Affairs)
The office that handles enrollment petitions, requirement waivers, academic standing questions, and confidential first conversations about accommodations.
DAS (Disability Access Services)
The office that provides accommodations for qualified students with disabilities. Renamed from the Disability Resource Center (DRC) in July 2024 -- DAS is the current name.
LSSC (Legal Skills in Social Context)
The 1L skills sequence (LAW 6160/6161/6165/6166), 2 credits each, 8 credits total across your first two terms.
Deficiency
NUSL's academic-standing unit. A Fail earns 2 deficiencies; a Marginal Pass earns 1. Accumulate 2 and you're on academic probation; 6+ in your first year can trigger dismissal.
Marginal Pass (MP)
A passing grade that still carries 1 deficiency -- credit is earned, but it counts against your academic-standing total differently than an ordinary Pass.
Co-op (Cooperative Education)
NUSL's required supervised legal work placement -- 6 credits for a 300-hour field placement plus 2 credits for the concurrent reflections seminar, typically ~20-21 hrs/week over a 14-week semester.
Rigorous Writing Requirement
A degree requirement satisfied by a revised, well-researched piece of at least 15 pages (3,750 words), certified by a supervising professor.
Public Interest Requirement
Satisfied by a public-interest co-op, one of NUSL's public interest clinics, 30 pre-approved uncompensated hours, or a directed study -- does not include judicial clerkships or private-firm work.
7-Year Completion Rule
ABA Standard 311(b) requires the JD be completed within 7 years of starting -- leaves of absence do not pause this clock.
1L / 2L / 3L / 4L
Shorthand for your year in the JD program -- first, second, third, fourth-year law student. FlexJD runs four years instead of the standard three because it's part-time.
SBA (Student Bar Association)
The elected student representative body at NUSL, affiliated with the ABA Law Student Division, and home to the dedicated Chair of FlexJD Interests seat.
FSA (FlexJD Student Association)
A FlexJD-specific student organization (registered 2026) built to give remote/part-time students their own community structure, separate from SBA.
SAP (Satisfactory Academic Progress)
The federal financial-aid standard students must meet to keep receiving aid -- a 67% completion-of-attempted-credits rule and a 150% maximum-timeframe rule, on top of NUSL's own good-standing requirement.
R2T4 (Return to Title IV)
The federal calculation that determines how much of your federal financial aid is considered "earned" if you withdraw mid-term -- fully earned once you've attended 60% of the term.
Grad PLUS
A federal graduate/professional loan program being eliminated for new borrowers on programs starting after June 30, 2026 under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Existing borrowers in an ongoing program can generally keep borrowing at current terms.
02

General Legal Vocabulary

Bar exam
The licensing exam required to practice law in a given state. Most states now use the UBE (Uniform Bar Exam), which allows score transfer between UBE jurisdictions.
UBE (Uniform Bar Exam)
A standardized bar exam used by most (not all) states, allowing a passing score to be transferred to any other UBE jurisdiction within that state's transfer window.
MPRE (Multistate Professional Responsibility Examination)
A separate, shorter exam on legal ethics required for bar admission in nearly every state, typically taken during law school.
Clerkship
A position working directly for a judge, researching and drafting opinions. Federal clerkships are hired through OSCAR; state clerkships vary by court.
OSCAR
The Online System for Clerkship Application and Review -- the centralized federal judiciary platform where students apply for federal judicial clerkships.
Externship
Supervised, often unpaid legal work for academic credit, usually at a government office, nonprofit, or court -- distinct from a co-op or clinic.
Clinic
A law school course where students represent real clients under a supervising attorney's license -- combines classroom instruction with live casework.
Pro bono
Legal work performed for free, typically for clients who can't afford representation.
Amicus brief
A "friend of the court" filing from someone not party to a case, offering the court additional argument or information relevant to the decision.
Voir dire
The jury selection process, where attorneys and the judge question prospective jurors to identify bias before trial.
Deposition
Sworn out-of-court testimony taken during discovery, recorded for potential use at trial.
Docket
A court's official log of case filings, hearings, and rulings -- also used loosely to mean a court or attorney's overall caseload.
Tort
A civil wrong (other than breach of contract) that causes harm, giving the injured party grounds to sue for damages -- negligence, defamation, and battery are all torts.
Common law
Law developed by courts through judicial decisions and precedent, as opposed to law created by statute.
Precedent
A previously decided case that guides or binds the outcome of later cases with similar facts, under the doctrine of stare decisis.
Reciprocity (bar admission)
An arrangement letting an attorney licensed in one state become licensed in another without retaking the full bar exam -- rules vary significantly by state and are not universal.
Bluebook
The standard legal citation manual used across most U.S. law schools and courts -- "Bluebooking" means formatting citations to its rules.
OCI (On-Campus Interviewing)
A structured recruiting process, mostly used by large law firms, where employers interview students on campus (or virtually) during a set application window each year.
